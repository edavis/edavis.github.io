Graphs and the y-axis

September 15, 2014   

For as long as I've been aware of this sort of thing, I've always been under the impression that it is wrong to create a graph where the y-axis doesn't start at zero.

And yet, here is a graph by Harry Enten at FiveThirtyEight that does exactly that:

I remember being told in elementary school that doing this exaggerates the difference between values and should be avoided.

Wikipedia seems to agree with me but also notes that it's okay to not start at zero when zero is basically an impossible value to have on the graph.

Zero percent is pretty far from these values, but it's not an impossible value.

I suppose 50% can be defended as it's 1/2 of 100% and a nice breakpoint. And if the purpose of a graph is to visualize numbers and/or the change in numbers, this isn't a terrible graph. It accurately reflects the decline from 84% to 70% which is all you can ask of it.

Who knows, maybe I'm mis-remembering the rule. I certainly see enough graphs that don't start at zero that it's probably not as hard-and-fast rule as I recall it being.
